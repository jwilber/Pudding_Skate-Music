The Good, the Rad, and the Gnarly

An exploration into the soundtrack of skateboarding.

By Jared Wilber & Matt Daniels

Since its earliest days, skateboarding has been intrinsically linked with music.

Many well-known artists have referenced skateboarding as a defining influence, and many popular songs explicitly mention the act skateboarding.

Culturally, skateboarding is associated with a large variety of musical styles, and while some styles have been more-or-less popular at different points in time, almost all have had a place at some point in skateboarding.

Skate videos showcase skateboarding from some company or group of friends. In general, each skater gets their own section in the video, with his/her own song.

The choice of song is very important, sometimes even more so than the skating itself. A good song sets the tempo of the video part; it matches with the skater's style and contributes to an overall aesthetic for the video. In contrast, a bad song ruins the experience, and results in an unremarkable video part, destined to be forgotten.

Growing up, like most skaters, if I wasn't skating, I was watching skate videos, discovering not only my new favourite skaters, but my new favourite music.

My first skate video, Blind's Video Days, turned me on to a diverse set of awesome artists, such as Dinosaur Jr., Black Flag, and John Coltrane.

In a time before Spotify or Pandora, skate videos provided something of a music tastemaker - a playlist of what's cool and who's hot in music. It spanned all genres, and all time-frames. It felt diverse. It felt counterculture. It felt cool.

In what follows, we'll learn how the music in skate videos tracks the evolution of the styles and trends in skateboarding over time. We'll also identify the most-used artists in skate videos, and explore the contents of the influential soundtrack of skateboarding.

Genre Evolution Over Time

As with any sufficiently developed sub-culture, skateboarding has gone through, and continues to go through, temporal shifts in styles and trends.

Particular music genres are often associated with particular styles in skateboarding. For example, hip hop is often used with technical street skating, while heavier, faster music is generally used with transition (think ramps and ppols).

It's not surprising, then, that the year-to-year changes in the genre of skate video's often reflects the year-to-year changes in the styles of skateboarding, serving as a vessel for communicating what was hot in that genre.

The Beginnings

Skate videos became an established feature of skateboarding culture in the later-half of the 1980s. For skateboarding, the 1980s were a pretty wild time; many DIY, skater-run companies were forming, vert skating, with its eclectic and show-man personalities, was in full popularity, and skateboarding was, for the first time, seeing commercial success. Early skate videos, borrowing from their counter-culture origins, were overwhelmingly dominated by punk rock music. Many of the artists featured in the first videos were bands formed by the skaters themselves, but early punk icons, such as Black Flag, The Faction, and Descendents, saw prominence as well. The iconic noise rock duo Sonic Youth, an artist heavily used throughout skateboarding, was first featured in a skate video in 1989, years before their mainstream success in the 1990s/2000s.

The 90's

In the 90’s, Skateboarding entered something of a hermit stage - it was no longer 'cool', and many aspects of the culture changed. Vert skating died, technical street skating took over, skateboarding entered the infamous era of ‘big pants, small wheels’[image link], and, of course, the music changed.
Hip Hop entered skating's discography for the first time and quickly filled the gap left by the large decline in punk rock music. Punk

Bad Brains, Minor Threat, Devo, Black Flag - those most-used artists in skateboarding reads like a whos-who of influential punk bands. Unsurprisingly, Fugazi leads the pack. Fugazi has been paired with some of skateboarding's most iconic skateboarders, and Ian Mackaye, Fugazi (and Minor Threat) lead singer, was at one point an avid skateboarder. Mackaye described the influence skateboarding had on his music, and detailed the contrast between the openness/accessibility of skateboarding and punk rock, with the closed off, exclusive culture of mainstream music. This sentiment is undoubtedly shared by many skateboarders-turned-musicians, and is likely a large reason indie, hip hop, and punk have come to dominate skateboarding to such an extent.

Despite the perceived differences between their primary audiences, Classic Rock is something of a parallel to skateboarding. Music can describe the time, origin, and culture of skateboarding.
